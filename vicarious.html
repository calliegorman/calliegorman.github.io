The problem with being a solo, long-term traveler

Solo travelers who are abroad for long periods of time can be faced with a major problem: ensuring family and friends that they will survive their adventure around the world and keep in touch while doing so.

But it is a difficult and time consuming task to update friends and family with details of travels on a daily basis through calls and texts.


How vicarious can help

The app decreases the amount of effort it would normally take for a user to update family and friends through traditional means, by asking a finite number of simple questions and prompts daily.

At the same time, it gives users’ family and friends an overview of what users are doing, where they are, and how they are feeling each day.

UX RESEARCH

User Research

The idea for vicarious arose from personal experience I had while traveling abroad solo for several months. My family wanted to hear from me frequently, but I didn’t always have the time or consistent wifi connection to do so.

While reflecting on this experience, I knew that an app like vicarious would have helped me immensely. The question was - would it help others in similar or different traveling circumstances?

Competitive Analysis

There are several apps on the market that help travelers keep in touch with family while abroad, but travelers may find difficulty in using the apps if they are not connected to wifi for long periods of time.

There are also several collaborative travel journal and planning apps that allow family and friends to follow a traveler’s itinerary or journals.

The problem is that these apps do not address the two issues vicarious is trying to solve.

1) Keep in constant communication with loved ones about daily activity

2) Decrease the amount of time daily to achieve that communication

Even though there is no travel app on the market exactly addressing the problems vicarious does, it was important to analyze the most popular itinerary and journaling apps given audiences may overlap.

A personal travel log app

Threat: Users love that the app automatically tracks your location and all the places you visit throughout the day.

Opportunity: Trip summaries are specific to geographic locations, number of steps, distance, and photos, but no other trip details.

A travel journal focused on travel maps

Threat: Beautiful, simple interface where friends on the same trip can collaborate on entries.

Opportunity: Focus is on writing open ended journal entries and uploading photos.

A collaborative travel itinerary app

Threat: One of the most well nown travel apps on the market

Opportunity: Interface feels outdated, and there is no place to add other info aside from itinerary

Compeitive Analysis contd.

The communication and time issues while traveling laid out by survey respondents, coupled with the fact that these travel itinerary and journal apps do not address those problems, led me to feel confident that vicarious had a chance to do well in the market.

VISUAL DESIGN & TESTING

High Fidelity Mockups

Next, I applied the branding elements to the wireframes to develop the high fidelity mockups. There were two major sections my process with the mockups:

1) I went through several iterations and updates in the design critique with seasoned designers and my design peers

2) After polishing the designs to a more finalized version, I ran several usability tests.

Design Critiques

I received pretty positive feedback but there were some significant changes that needed to be made to meet the Human Interface Guidelines. These two changes positively influenced the user experience of the app.

The “take a tour” pop up box on the homescreen moved around the screen in order to closely identify each icon the tip referenced. After feedback suggesting I review the Human Interface Guidelines. I found that the alert box is meant to stay in one spot in the middle of the screen, featuring the item of focus on top of a blurred background. This change made the whole experience far more seamless and readable.

Alert pop ups before feedback

Alert pop ups after feedback

Design Critiques contd.

Several screens within the app are featured as cards that pop up from the bottom of the screen. My initial design drafts featured the bottom tab bar when these cards appeared. I received feedback from my peers that this did not meet the Human Interface Guidelines, and overall was a poor experience to provide users the option to click within the tab bar while in a card pop up.

“Add contact” screen pre user feedback, which featured too many possible user actions.


“Add contact” screen post user feedback, featuring the pop up card overlaying the full screen.

Usability Tests

After updating the designs once again, I ran several four usability tests on the prototype, and received candid and helpful feedback from the users as they navigated the app.

There were two major updates made that bettered the user experience overall and also several smaller changes I made to the design based on the user feedback.


Highlight the icons during the intro tips

I received feedback that some of the icons that are meant to stand out during the “tips” section were too difficult to see. Now each icon is highlighted with a white circle so they stand out while users are reading through the app tips.

Before test


After test

Usability Tests contd.

Remove modals overlaying card pop ups

In several cases during the “add an entry” card screen, users are able to enter text. Initially, these were text boxes that appeared on top of the card as a pop up. I received feedback from two users that this did not seem like the right way a text box would appear in an application. Although it was not clear what the correct usage was in the Human Interface Guidelines, there is no evidence that a text pop up over a card is acceptable. Because of this and also given the feedback from the users, I removed the pop up and made the text box available in a screen within the card.

CONCLUSION

Takeaways

Overall, vicarious succeeded in being an app that could be useful for travelers who struggle with needing to keep in contact with loved ones at home.

I received feedback in design critiques and during usability tests that the concept for the app was unique and would be useful for the given audience. Even so, there are several things I would include in another prototype if given more time, such as:

  • Giving the user more flexibility with the information shared each day
  • Building out the full view from a viewer’s POV of the app (not just the traveler)
  • Adding a portion to the app where travelers could discover other nearby travelers

The most crucial thing I learned is the vast amount of information on the Human Interface Guidelines. In the end, the final app looks far more professional than it did in early designs. I am grateful for all of the feedback from people who pushed me to question each part of the design to ensure it met the correct guidelines. This information will only help improve my designs of iOS apps moving forward.
