The problem with being a solo, long-term traveler

Solo travelers who are abroad for long periods of time are faced with a major problem: ensuring family and friends that they will survive their adventure around the world and keep in touch while doing so.

But it is a difficult and time consuming task to update friends and family with details of travels on a daily basis through calls and texts.


How vicarious can help

The app decreases the amount of effort it would normally take for a user to update family and friends through traditional means, by asking a finite number of simple questions and prompts daily.

At the same time, it gives users’ family and friends an overview of what users are doing, where they are, and how they are feeling each day.

UX RESEARCH

User Research

The idea for vicarious arose from personal experience I had while traveling abroad solo for several months. My family wanted to hear from me frequently, but I didn’t always have the time or consistent wifi connection to do so.

While reflecting on this experience, I knew that an app like vicarious would have helped me immensely. The question was - would it help others in similar or different traveling circumstances?

56% of survey respondents described themselves as long-term travelers (have taken a trip solo for more than 1 month). Of that group:

86%

of travelers said they had never used a collaborative travel journal app

100%

of them said they do experience family and friends wanting to keep in touch with them while they travel.

93%

say the most prevalent reason why family and friends want to keep in touch is to ensure the traveler is safe.

65%

of respondents said they feel under pressure to keep in touch when they don’t have the time or wifi connection.

User Research Conclusion

Through this research, it was clear that long-term travelers were better candidates for the app because of safety and wellbeing factors.

Given the demographic of people surveyed, the likely user is probably a female who is traveling solo for a long period of time.

Next up was to analyze the competitive landscape to see if other apps were already addressing these issues.

Competitive Analysis

There are several apps on the market that help travelers keep in touch with family while abroad, in addition to collaborative travel journal and planning apps that allow family and friends to follow a traveler’s itinerary or journals.

The problem is that these apps do not address the two issues vicarious is trying to solve.


1) Keep in constant communication with loved ones about daily activity without constant wifi connection

2) Decrease the amount of time daily to achieve that communication


It was still important to research potential competitors where audiences might overlap:

A personal travel log app

Threat: Users love that the app automatically tracks your location and all the places you visit throughout the day.

Opportunity: Trip summaries are specific to geographic locations, number of steps, distance, and photos, but no other trip details.

A travel journal focused on travel maps

Threat: Beautiful, simple interface where friends on the same trip can collaborate on entries.

Opportunity: Focus is on writing open ended journal entries and uploading photos.

A collaborative travel itinerary app

Threat: One of the most well nown travel apps on the market

Opportunity: Interface feels outdated, and there is no place to add other info aside from itinerary

Compeitive Analysis Conclusion

The communication and time issues while traveling laid out by survey respondents, coupled with the fact that these travel itinerary and journal apps do not address those problems, led me to feel confident that vicarious had a chance to do well in the market.

User Personas

Interviews were held with three potential users.

The first two female users had experiences and goals that overlapped in several ways. These users exuded the characteristics of likely typical users of the app.

I luckily had the chance to interview a male user who did not experience the problems our typical users experience during traveling, but who still found that vicarious could be of use to him.

Logo Development

Several sketches were completed to come up with a logo that would show this was a travel application.

After several preference tests, it was clear that people enjoyed a simple location marker with “vicarious” written underneath. This was perfect because it focused on the part of travel that family and friends want to know about their traveler - where he or she is at all times.

CONCLUSION

What I learned

Overall, vicarious succeeded in being an app that could be useful for travelers who struggle with needing to keep in contact with loved ones at home.

Given more time, there are several things I would include in a more extensive prototype:

  • Giving the user more flexibility with the information shared each day
  • Building out the full view from a viewer’s POV of the app (not just the traveler)
  • Adding a portion to the app where travelers could discover other nearby travelers

The most crucial thing I learned is the vast amount of information on the Human Interface Guidelines. In the end, the final app looks far more professional than it did in early designs. I am grateful for all of the feedback from people who pushed me to question each part of the design to ensure it met the correct guidelines. This information will only help improve my designs of iOS apps moving forward.
